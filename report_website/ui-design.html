UI Design

Design Principles

Our research and requirement pinpointing led to a general idea of the user base for our product. It was then determined that the user interface (UI) was quintessential to our users' experience with our project. As progress was made and our requirements developed, our UI design stayed consistent with Nielsen's Ten Heuristics for User Interface Design[1]. The main heuristics we abided are:

Aesthetic and minimalist design

As our project had a focus on elderly users, we focused our UI design on simplicity and usability. We prioritised large icons and text for ease of reading, and made use of simple words for the users to understand.

Recognition rather than recall

Another important design principle we followed is the importance of an obvious UI. This included hiding the technical system behind a simplified UI with clear buttons for navigation. We also attach instructions in the form of user manuals and in-app instructions where necessary.

We use simplistic words to cater to users of all technical backgrounds. Our chatbot was also prompted to reply in simple terms, keeping every interaction natural and flowing.

[1] J. Nielsen, "10 Heuristics for User Interface Design," Nielsen Norman Group, Jan. 30, 2024. https://www.nngroup.com/articles/ten-usability-heuristics/

Sketches

After pinpointing our users' requirements and identifying our design principles, our team started out with designing the basic sketch of our product. We focused our sketch on a minimalistic interface, allowing users to focus on the conversation. The large avatars also make interactions feel natural, resembling a conversation with a real person. First Wireframe

After refining our sketches and agreeing on a general design, we set out with designing the wireframes. Our wireframe illustrates a high-level idea of the interaction between user and program. Experimenting with the wireframe allowed us to refine our idea of the UI, and develop new requirements that fit our project.

Evaluation

To evaluate our wireframe, a heuristic evaluation was set up, with Nielsen's Ten Heuristic Evaluations forming our reference. We chose a heuristic evaluation due to the limited resources at our disposal. Regardless, the evaluation proved important to determining the key gaps in our design.

Heuristic Problem Solution Severity
Error prevention Only audio-based input and feedback are used, which may cause issues for users if they misheard or pronounced something incorrectly. Also prevents users with hearing and speaking disabilities from using the device Added visual written feedback and a clickable user interface 3
User control and freedom Lack of an undo and redo function options in the menus Added an undo and redo option in all menus 4
Flexibility and efficiency of use The chatbox should provide a chat history for users to reference past chats Added a chatbox history function 2

Final UI

Our final UI changed a lot from our initial sketches and wireframes. As our project grew and new requirements were discovered, our design adapted, albeit still heavily inspired by our initial brainstorming. Our final design closely follows the ten heuristic practices set by Nielsen, specifically the three design practices we highlighted earlier.
